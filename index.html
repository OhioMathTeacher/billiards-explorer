Billiards & Reflection

Maryam Mirzakhani won the 2014 Fields Medal in part for her work on billiard trajectories on flat surfaces. Her central technique — unfolding a polygon by reflecting it instead of the ball — turns a zig-zag path into a straight line, and reveals deep connections between geometry, dynamics, and topology. These sketches let you explore that idea directly.

MIRA Golf — Equilateral Triangle

Drag a tee and target inside an equilateral triangle. A slider reveals all hole-in-one billiard paths for 0–4 bounces; click any path to isolate it. Toggle Show unfolding to see the reflected triangle copies extend outward — one per bounce — and watch the zig-zag path become a straight line.

Mirzakhani connection. The equilateral triangle is a rational polygon, so unfolding it produces a flat torus. Billiard paths become geodesics on that surface. Mirzakhani proved that the number of closed trajectories of length ≤ L grows like L² — a landmark counting theorem whose proof depends on exactly the unfolding technique this sketch makes visible.

Grid Billiards Explorer

Launch a ball from a corner of an m × n table at a chosen slope (rise/run) and watch it bounce, animated step by step. The table dimensions and slope together determine which pocket the ball reaches — or whether it ever pockets at all. GCD, parity, and angle all play a role.

Mirzakhani connection. Tiling the plane with reflected copies of the rectangle turns the bouncing path into a straight line — a geodesic on a flat torus. This is the simplest instance of the unfolding principle Mirzakhani generalized to every rational polygon. When GCD(m, n) = 1, the geodesic is dense; otherwise it is periodic. Both behaviors appear in her broader theory of translation surfaces.
