Billiards & Reflection

TCE 265 — Mathematics, History & Technology · Miami University

In 2014, Maryam Mirzakhani became the first woman — and the first Iranian — to receive the Fields Medal, mathematics’ highest honor. A central thread in her work was billiard trajectories on flat surfaces: the study of how a ball bouncing inside a polygon traces paths whose complexity conceals profound geometric order. Her key technique was unfolding — instead of reflecting the ball, reflect the table. A zig-zag billiard path becomes a straight line in the unfolded picture, linking combinatorics, geometry, and the deep theory of moduli spaces. The interactive sketches below let you explore that idea directly.

MIRA Golf — Equilateral Triangle Explorer

Drag a tee and target anywhere inside an equilateral triangle. Find every hole-in-one billiard path for 0–4 bounces, isolate individual paths, and reveal the geometric unfolding behind each one.

Using the reflection principle, the applet finds all valid paths by “unfolding” the target: reflect it across each candidate wall, then draw a straight line back to the tee. The bounce point appears where that line crosses the wall. For multi-bounce paths, the target is reflected through a sequence of walls in reverse order. Toggle Show unfolding to watch the reflected triangles and straight lines appear — one reflected copy per bounce, extending outward from the original table.

Mirzakhani connection. The equilateral triangle is a rational polygon (all angles are rational multiples of π), which means repeatedly unfolding it produces a finite flat surface — in fact, a torus. Billiard paths in the triangle correspond to geodesics on that torus. Mirzakhani’s early work studied the counting problem: how many closed billiard trajectories of length ≤ L exist, and how does that number grow with L? Her answer — that the count grows like L² with an explicit constant tied to the geometry of the surface — was a landmark result. The unfolding visualization in this applet is the same technique at the heart of that theory.

Grid Billiards Explorer

Launch a ball from a corner of an m × n rectangular table and watch it bounce until it reaches a pocket — or keeps going. Set the rise and run of the launch angle to control the slope.

The ball travels at a fixed slope (rise/run) and reflects off walls with equal angles of incidence and reflection. The table dimensions and the slope together determine everything: which pocket the ball reaches, how many bounces it takes, and whether it ever pockets at all. Key patterns emerge quickly — the role of GCD(m, n), parity of the dimensions, and what happens at a 45° angle. The animated path builds bounce-by-bounce; the slope landmark shows the first lattice point on the trajectory.

Mirzakhani connection. The rectangle is the simplest case of the unfolding story. Tiling the plane with reflected copies of the rectangle turns the bouncing ball into a straight line in the plane — a geodesic on a flat torus. Mirzakhani’s work on flat surfaces generalizes this idea to every rational polygon: each produces a translation surface, and straight-line flows on those surfaces have the same equidistribution and counting properties she proved. The rectangle case makes the core idea tangible: GCD(m, n) = 1 forces the geodesic to be dense, while larger GCD values produce periodic orbits.
